01

What is Interactive Storytelling?

A story where the audience doesn’t just watch — it decides. Narrative as system, not sequence.

Defining features:

  • The reader/player is an agent, not a spectator
  • Choices have consequences that alter the narrative
  • Multiple valid paths through the same story world
  • The author designs a possibility space, not a fixed text

Forms include:

  • Video games & text adventures
  • Hypertext fiction & Twine games
  • Interactive film & VR experiences
  • Physical installations & immersive theater
  • Chatbots, AI narrative engines

If the audience can change the story — who is the author?

Textual Structures

From linear to multilinear — how narrative paths diverge

LINEAR

A fixed sequence of words. Read conventionally from beginning to end.

NONLINEAR

All texts that do not follow Aristotelian laws. The user controls the order.

MULTILINEAR

User-defined pathways branching into different narrative arcs and endings.

Chatbots

From Eliza to Galatea — the evolution of conversational AI

ELIZA — 1966

Joseph Weizenbaum’s pioneering chatbot at MIT:

  • Simulated psychotherapeutic conversations
  • Used keyword recognition and pattern matching
  • Responded with predetermined phrases
  • Created a convincing illusion of understanding

“The first machine that made humans forget they were talking to a machine — a distinction we would spend the next 60 years unsuccessfully trying to restore.”

GALATEA — 2000

Emily Short’s landmark interactive fiction:

  • Recognizes and adapts to user input
  • Builds unique narratives in real-time
  • Maintains context awareness
  • Remembers past interactions

Key innovation: Personalized collaborative storytelling between AI and humans.

Hypertext as a Lens

Ted Nelson coined the term in 1963. The idea: text need not be linear. Links are not just navigation — they are editorial acts. Every choice of what to connect is an argument about meaning.

Non-linear Narrative

The reader navigates, and in navigating, authors their own reading. The text changes depending on the path taken. There is no canonical version.

The Link as Argument

Every hyperlink is a rhetorical move. Where you place a link — and where it leads — shapes interpretation. The structure is the narrative.

Reader Agency

Freedom to explore — but within a space the author designed. The illusion of infinite choice inside a carefully bounded world. Sound familiar?

07
Spatial Hypertext · Body · Identity

Patchwork Girl

“She is buried in several places.”

Shelley Jackson’s Patchwork Girl (1995) is one of the most studied works in digital literature. Composed in Storyspace, it retells Frankenstein from the monster’s perspective — except the monster is a woman stitched together from female corpses.

The structure is the argument: each lexia is a body part, each link a suture. To read the text is to assemble the body. You cannot read it linearly because the body was never linear — it was collected, scattered, buried in several places.

  • Five sections: The Body · A Graveyard · A Journal · A Story · Broken Accents
  • Intertextual with Mary Shelley, L. Frank Baum, and Donna Haraway’s cyborg
  • Spatial hypertext: Storyspace’s map shows the “body map” of nodes
  • First major hypertext by a woman — and the most formally ambitious of its era

Escape Rooms — Physical Multilinearity

An escape room is a spatially-authored branching narrative: the players decide what to explore, in what order, with what collaborators. The puzzle is the plot. There is no single reading — only a shared attempt at one. Physical multilinearity, sold by the hour.

Analog Interactivity

Gamebooks

Interactive analog texts where choices impact the narrative. Puzzles, combat, and challenges determine the story’s path.

We Are Muesli (Milan) — Narrative games for cultural heritage since 2013.

House of Leaves — Mark Z. Danielewski, 2000

A novel about a house that is bigger on the inside than the outside — and a book that enacts this structurally. Footnotes that devour the text, appendices that contradict the narrative, multiple unreliable editors. The page itself becomes a labyrinth. Physical hypertext before anyone called it that.

Ergodic Literature — Espen Aarseth, 1997

From the Greek ergon (work) + hodos (path): literature that requires non-trivial effort to traverse. Aarseth gave us the vocabulary to say why the I Ching and MUDs belong in the same sentence — and why reading a novel and navigating a hypertext are structurally different acts.

Participatory Culture · Remix · Collective Authorship

Memes & the Distributed Narrative

The meme is the folk tale of the digital age — collectively authored, endlessly remixed, and fundamentally untraceable. Which is, depending on your perspective, either democracy or chaos.

Henry Jenkins — Participatory Culture

A culture with low barriers to expression, strong support for sharing, and informal mentorship. The audience is no longer passive: it produces.

The Meme as Narrative Unit

Every meme format encodes a story grammar: setup, subversion, punchline. The Drake Format, "This is Fine", Distracted Boyfriend — each one is a reusable narrative template.

Remix Culture & Intertextuality

Meaning is produced through recombination. A meme divorced from its original context generates new readings. Lawrence Lessig called it “Read/Write culture” — as opposed to a Read-Only one.

Small Web · Indie Web · Digital Sovereignty

The Handmade Web

Before the platform swallowed the page, the web was a place where anyone could build a room of their own. Some people never stopped building.

From GeoCities to Neocities

GeoCities (1994–2009) hosted over 38 million user-built pages. When Yahoo shut it down, a community archived it. Neocities (2013) revived the spirit: free hosting, raw HTML, no algorithm.

Against the Walled Garden

Social platforms offer reach in exchange for control. The handmade web offers the opposite: zero reach, total autonomy. It is the zine culture of the internet — distributed, personal, intentionally small.

J.R. Carpenter — “A Handmade Web” (2015)

“I evoke the term handmade web to suggest slowness and smallness as a forms of resistance.” The personal webpage as narrative act: a space authored, curated, and inhabited by a single voice.

Digital Aesthetics · Net Culture · Narrative Form

Aesthetics as Storytelling

Every aesthetic movement encodes a worldview — and a narrative. These are not genres of music or image styles. They are survival strategies for living inside digital culture. (click slide to toggle glitch)

VAPORWAVE — 2010–

Grief for futures that never arrived. Vaporwave aestheticizes the wreckage of late capitalism — elevator music, Windows 95, mall architecture — and turns nostalgia into narrative. A E S T H E T I C as performance.

Macintosh Plus, 2814, Chuck Person — the album as interactive memory palace.

GLITCH ART — Error as Grammar

Rosa Menkman: “A glitch is a wonderful window into the machine.” Glitch art weaponizes system failure as a narrative of disruption. When the interface breaks, ideology becomes visible.

Cory Arcangel, JODI, net.art — error as authorial choice.

Also: glitchology.com — a taxonomy of glitch aesthetics.

HYPERPOP — 2019–

SOPHIE, 100 gecs, A.G. Cook — PC Music. Maximalist digital production as narrative overload. The sound of being online at 4am. Every element pushed to saturation: the interface as overwhelm.

DEEP FRIED FEELS

Emotional irony through compression artifacts. Deep-fried aesthetics (extreme JPEG noise, oversaturation, distortion) create a second layer of reading: the content says one thing, the damage says another.

It is the unreliable narrator of image formats. Distortion becomes sincerity by other means — the meme so compressed it loops back to feeling.

“Deep fried feels” — irony as an aesthetic survival tool in digital culture.

EXIT REALITY

The desire to leave. Exit Reality names a recurring theme across VR art, net art, game culture, and digital fiction: the screen as threshold, the avatar as escape hatch, the world as optional.

From Second Life to VRChat to Ready Player One to Fortnite concerts — the interactive narrative that promises: you can be elsewhere.

Critical question: who gets to exit — and what are they exiting into?

Façade

Façade (2005) by Michael Mateas & Andrew Stern — the first full-length interactive drama driven by a live Drama Manager. You visit a couple, you type freely, the AI responds in real time. It can throw you out.

Natural language input. No menus, no buttons. Type anything — the system interprets intent.

Key Innovation

Platform Studies · Algorithmic Culture · Narrative Architecture

Platformization & the Algorithmic Narrator

The most influential storyteller of the 21st century has no name, no face, and no artistic intent. It is an algorithm — and it decides what you see next.

TikTok / Reels

Avg. video: 21–34 seconds. The platform rewards a specific narrative arc: hook in 2s → conflict → payoff. Creators who break this structure disappear from the feed.

“The For You Page is a Drama Manager that optimises for retention, not meaning.”

YouTube

70% of watch time comes from autoplay recommendations. YouTube’s algorithm is the world’s largest serialized narrative engine: it decides the next episode of your life.

Rabbit holes as unintentional branching narratives.

Instagram

Organic reach: <10% of followers. The Stories format (24h ephemeral) introduced narrative scarcity to social media. Miss it and the story is gone — FOMO as narrative mechanism.

The timeline as curated autobiography.

José van Dijck — The Culture of Connectivity (2013)

Platforms are not neutral pipelines. They have embedded values, business models, and defaults that shape what stories get told and which ones vanish.

The Attention Economy

Herbert Simon (1971): “A wealth of information creates a poverty of attention.” Platform narratives are optimized not for depth or truth, but for the single metric that funds them: time on screen.

Extended Reality · Embodied Narrative · Presence

AR/VR & Immersive Storytelling

When the story surrounds you on all sides and responds to your body, the line between audience and protagonist dissolves. Whether this is liberation or vertigo depends on the day.

Notes on Blindness: Into Darkness (2016)

Based on John Hull’s audio diaries. VR experience of blindness through spatial sound and haptics. Narrative delivered without images — the medium itself enacts the theme.

Punchdrunk — Sleep No More (2011–)

Immersive theater in a 5-floor building. Audience wears masks, moves freely, constructs their own version of Macbeth. No two people experience the same story. Physical multilinearity.

Pokémon Go (2016)

AR as location-triggered narrative: the city becomes a story map. 500M+ downloads. First mainstream proof that the world itself can be a game board.

Presence & Embodied Agency

The defining feature of VR narrative is presence — the sensation of “being there.” Chris Milk called VR “the empathy machine.” Researchers have since complicated that claim considerably.

The Emerging Impact of AI

AI doesn’t just accelerate interactive storytelling — it fundamentally reframes the question of authorship. The machine can now generate the possibility space as you explore it. This is either exciting or terrifying. Possibly both.

NARRATIVE CREATION

LLMs can generate branching narratives, adapt tone to player choices, and maintain character consistency across thousands of interactions. The Drama Manager of the 1990s is now a foundation model.

  • Infinite story branches, generated live
  • NPC dialogue that remembers context
  • Real-time narrative adaptation
WRITER ASSISTANCE

For interactive fiction authors, AI becomes a co-writer, continuity editor, and stress-tester — identifying plot holes across hundreds of narrative branches humans can’t track simultaneously.

  • Plot consistency across branches
  • Character voice maintenance
  • Automated playtesting
THE OPEN QUESTION

If the story is generated in response to you, is it yours? If the AI adapts to your choices, is there still an author — or only a mirror?

“The hallucination is not a bug. It’s the only honest answer to an impossible question.”

AI Drama Manager — or, what happens when the system writes back

The Drama Manager is the invisible author inside the machine. It reads the story, reads the player, and bends one toward the other. AI becomes the authorial layer that the original Drama Manager was only simulating.

AI Dungeon — 2019

Nick Walton’s GPT-2 experiment that became a product. The model doesn’t manage drama — it improvises it. Every response is a bet on what the story probably does next.

Fascinating for what it gets right accidentally. Terrifying for what it gets wrong with complete confidence.

Drama Manager — the concept

James Lester, 1997. A system monitoring narrative state to maintain tension, pacing, and character arc. Running in real time, editing what the player experiences.

The question it asks: what should happen next, given everything that has already happened? Which is, incidentally, the same question every writer asks at 2am.

Educational Focus

Interactive storytelling in education: a structural shift from telling students what happened to putting them inside a situation where it matters. The difference is the difference between a map and a journey.

Identity & Agency

Choices carry consequence. Students rehearse decision-making in a space where failure is allowed — and instructive.

Transversal Skills

Creativity, collaboration, critical thinking — not as listed objectives, but as the mechanics of the experience itself.

Metacognition

After a branching narrative, students can trace their path. They learn to ask: why did I choose that? What did I assume?

Emotional Intelligence

Perspective-taking through character embodiment. Empathy as a design mechanic, not a virtue lecture.

80 Days — Inkle Studios

80 Days (2014) — Inkle Studios. A global branching narrative with 150+ cities, used in secondary education worldwide for geography, history and ethics.

CONCLUSION

What we've learned

“Interactive storytelling: a relationship between author, system, and reader. One that has been redesigned, contested, and reinvented every decade for fifty years — and shows no signs of settling down.”

From Colossal Cave to AI Dungeon, from Storyspace to Neocities, from MYST to Bandersnatch: the question has always been the same. What does it mean to give the audience a choice?

The answer changes depending on who asks — programmer, artist, activist, platform, algorithm. The history of interactive storytelling is the history of that negotiation.

The story is not over. It never was. You just reached a branch point.
